Book of Moses

In this book you will learn about three great principles

The Vision of Moses

In the Pearl of Great Price, Moses receives a profound vision that expands his understanding of divine reality and his own calling. Through this revelation, he learns three central truths. First, he comes to understand the nature of God as a living, personal, and purposeful being whose work is centered on the immortality and eternal life of humanity. Second, Moses gains insight into the nature of divine sonship, recognizing Jesus Christ as the Only Begotten Son and Messiah, through whom God’s plan is accomplished and through whom humanity may become sons and daughters of God. Finally, Moses learns the nature of his own mission, discovering that he has been chosen and prepared to participate in God’s work by leading, teaching, and covenanting with His people. This vision establishes Moses’s identity, authority, and purpose, grounding his prophetic mission in a broader understanding of God’s eternal plan.

The Creation of Earth - God's Nature

In his vision of the Creation recorded in the Pearl of Great Price, Moses is shown God’s role as the divine Creator who brings order, purpose, and meaning to the universe. Through this revelation, Moses learns that the creation of the earth was not a random or purely physical event, but a deliberate act guided by God’s wisdom and eternal purposes. God is revealed as a being who creates with intent, establishing laws, boundaries, and conditions that allow life to flourish and humanity to progress. This vision emphasizes God’s nature as purposeful and relational, creating the earth as a prepared place where His children may exercise agency, form covenants, and advance toward eternal life. The Creation narrative thus reinforces that God’s power is inseparably connected to His loving purpose for humanity.

The Fall - Sonship's Nature

In the account of the Fall of Adam found in the Pearl of Great Price, Moses learns that the Fall was not merely a transgression, but a necessary step in God’s plan that revealed the redemptive role of Jesus Christ as the Messiah. Through the choices of Adam and Eve, humanity entered a mortal state marked by growth, agency, and accountability. Adam’s progression throughout the Fall demonstrates a movement from innocence to understanding, as he comes to recognize the purpose of mortality and the need for redemption through Christ. Eve likewise acts with wisdom and faith, enabling the continuation of God’s plan. Before leaving the Garden of Eden, Adam and Eve are clothed in coats of skins, a powerful symbol of Jesus Christ’s atoning sacrifice, signifying that redemption and protection from spiritual death come only through Him. This act foreshadows Christ’s role as the Savior, whose sacrifice covers humanity’s fallen condition and makes possible reconciliation with God.

The Creation of Zion - Our divine Mission

In Moses’s vision of Enoch, he learns profound truths about the nature of his own divine mission. Enoch, who initially describes himself as “slow of speech,” is nevertheless called by God to preach repentance to a fallen world, demonstrating that divine authority does not depend on human eloquence but on obedience and faith. As Enoch fulfills his stewardship, he is entrusted with the responsibility to care for others by proclaiming the gospel and inviting all to return to God. Through this vision, Moses comes to understand that his mission similarly requires compassion, courage, and unwavering commitment to the welfare of God’s children. God’s deep love and mercy are revealed as He weeps for the lost and continually extends opportunities for repentance, showing that His work is motivated not by judgment alone, but by a profound desire to redeem and gather His children. This vision affirms that Moses’s calling is rooted in service, stewardship, and the sacred duty to labor for the salvation of others.
